Chapter 1

The Inheritance

The letter arrived on a grey Tuesday morning, delivered by a man in an old-fashioned coat who disappeared before Catherine could ask him any questions. The envelope was yellowed with age, sealed with wax that bore a rose in full bloom.

Inside, the handwriting was elegant but faded: "My dearest Catherine, by the time you read this, I will be with the roses. The garden is yours now. Guard it well, for it holds more than beauty. Come at sunset on the summer solstice, and all will be revealed. Your loving grandmother, Eleanor."

Catherine held the letter to her chest, tears blurring her vision. Grandmother Eleanor had passed three months ago, and Catherine had thought all the grieving was done. But this message from beyond the grave reopened the wound even as it offered something precious—a final connection to the woman who had raised her.

The summer solstice was tomorrow.

Catherine had not been to Thornwood Manor since she was a child. Her father had forbidden it after a quarrel with Eleanor that he never explained. But her father was gone now too, and there was no one left to stop her from claiming her inheritance.

She packed a small trunk and took the morning train into the countryside. The journey took her through rolling hills and sleepy villages, past fields of lavender and wheat. As the train pulled into Thornwood station, Catherine felt as though she was traveling not just through space, but through time itself.

Thornwood Manor stood at the end of a long, tree-lined drive, its grey stone walls covered in climbing roses. The house looked exactly as Catherine remembered—elegant, slightly wild, full of secrets. She paid the driver and stood before the iron gates, hesitating.

The roses were in full bloom, hundreds of them in every shade from white to deepest crimson. Their scent filled the air, sweet and almost dizzying. Catherine pushed open the gate and it swung silently inward, as if welcoming her home.

The house keys had been in the letter's envelope, and they worked perfectly despite their apparent age. Inside, everything was covered in dust sheets, but Catherine could see the shapes of familiar furniture beneath them. She wandered through rooms filled with memories, each one awakening something she had forgotten she knew.

But it was the garden that called to her.

She found it through the French doors in what had been Eleanor's sitting room. The rose garden spread out before her like something from a dream—carefully tended beds arranged in intricate patterns, a fountain at the center with a statue of a woman holding a rose. The garden was immaculate, as if someone had been caring for it every day, though the house had stood empty for months.

Catherine walked the paths as the afternoon waned, reading the small plaques that identified each variety: Peace, Moonlight Serenade, Eternal Promise, Time's Embrace. The names seemed chosen with care, telling a story she didn't yet understand.

As the sun began to set, painting the sky in shades of rose and gold, Catherine stood before the fountain. The summer solstice—this was the moment her grandmother had specified. She waited, barely breathing, as the light faded and the first stars appeared.

The fountain began to glow.

It was subtle at first, just a shimmer in the water, but it grew brighter until the entire fountain was suffused with soft, golden light. The roses around her seemed to lean in, their petals catching the glow, and Catherine heard it—music, faint and beautiful, coming from nowhere and everywhere.

Then she saw him.

A man stood on the other side of the fountain, where no one had been a moment before. He was perhaps thirty, dressed in clothes from another era—a dark coat, a high collar, boots that had gone out of fashion a century ago. But it was his face that captured her—handsome and sad, with eyes that held lifetimes of longing.

"You came," he said, his voice rough with emotion. "She said you would, but I hardly dared to hope."

Catherine's voice caught in her throat. "Who... who are you?"

He smiled, and it transformed his face. "My name is James. And I have been waiting for you, Catherine, for longer than you can imagine."

The roses around them seemed to sing in the twilight, and Catherine realized that her grandmother's garden held more than beauty, just as the letter had promised. It held magic, mystery, and perhaps—she looked at the man across from her, at the hope in his eyes—it held love.

The summer solstice sunset painted them both in gold, and Catherine took her first step toward a truth that would change everything.
