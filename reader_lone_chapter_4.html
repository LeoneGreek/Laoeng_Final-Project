Chapter 1

The Woman in Black

Rain hammered against the window of my fifth-floor office like it had a personal grudge. I was nursing my third bourbon of the evening and contemplating my fourth when she walked in—the kind of woman who makes you forget your own name and remember every mistake you ever made.

She wore black from head to toe: a dress that probably cost more than my annual rent, gloves that went past her elbows, and a hat with a veil that obscured most of her face. But I could see her eyes through that veil—green as money, sharp as broken glass.

"Mr. Rook," she said, her voice like smoke and honey. "I need your help."

I gestured to the chair across from my desk. "That's what I'm here for, Miss...?"

"You can call me Mrs. Winters." She sat down with the kind of grace they don't teach, the kind you're born with or you're not. "Though I doubt that's my real name anymore."

I raised an eyebrow. "Care to explain that?"

She removed her gloves slowly, deliberately. Her hands were shaking. "Three days ago, I woke up in a hotel room with no memory of how I got there. My purse contained ten thousand dollars in cash, a revolver, and a key to a safety deposit box. There was blood on my dress. And in the bathroom..." her voice faltered, "in the bathroom was a dead man I'd never seen before."

I'd been a detective for fifteen years. I'd seen wives murder husbands, husbands murder wives, lovers, enemies, strangers. But something about the way she told the story made the hair on the back of my neck stand up.

"Did you call the police?"

"No." She met my eyes through the veil. "Because when I looked in the mirror, Mr. Rook, I didn't recognize my own reflection."

Now that was new.

"Amnesia?" I suggested, though I didn't believe it even as I said it.

"Perhaps. Or perhaps something worse." She opened her purse—expensive leather, monogrammed with initials that weren't M.W.—and pulled out a newspaper clipping. "This was in my coat pocket."

I took it. The headline read: "Socialite Margaret Winters Reported Missing." Below was a photograph of a woman who looked nothing like the woman sitting across from me.

"According to that article," she said, "I've been missing for three weeks. But I only lost three days. And I don't look like that woman. I don't sound like her. I don't have her memories."

"So either you're not Margaret Winters—"

"Or Margaret Winters isn't who I thought she was." She pulled out another item: a leather-bound journal. "I found this in the safety deposit box. It's written in my handwriting—or at least, in the handwriting of this body I'm wearing. But the things it describes... Mr. Rook, if this journal is true, I'm involved in something that makes murder look like a parking ticket."

She slid the journal across the desk. I opened it to a random page and started reading. My blood went cold.

The entries described meetings with city officials, exchanges of money, blackmail material on judges and politicians. There were names—big names, the kind that appeared in the society pages and ran this city from their mansions and private clubs. And running through it all like a dark thread was mention of something called "The Assembly."

"What is this?" I asked, though part of me didn't want to know.

"That's what I'm paying you to find out." She produced a bank envelope and set it on the desk. I didn't need to open it to know it was thick with bills. "I need to know who I am. Who Margaret Winters really was. What The Assembly is. And—" her voice dropped to a whisper, "I need to know if I killed that man in the hotel room."

I should have said no. Every instinct I'd honed over fifteen years on the job was screaming at me to walk away, to pour another bourbon and forget this woman with her impossible story and her dangerous questions.

But those green eyes were looking at me through that veil, and I've always been a sucker for a dame in distress.

"I'll take the case," I said. "But I work my way, which means I go where the evidence leads, even if you don't like where that is."

"I wouldn't have come to you if I wanted comfortable lies, Mr. Rook. The police are looking for Margaret Winters. Powerful people are looking for Margaret Winters. I need someone who isn't afraid to look for the truth."

She stood to leave, and I stood with her. "Where can I reach you?"

"You can't. I'll contact you. Every day at noon, I'll call. If I miss a call..." she didn't finish the sentence.

"One more thing," I said as she reached the door. "What do you want me to call you? If you're not Margaret Winters."

She paused, and I saw her shoulders sag slightly under the weight of all those unanswered questions. "I don't know who I am anymore," she said quietly. "So Margaret will have to do, until we find out the truth."

Then she was gone, leaving only the scent of expensive perfume and the lingering feeling that I'd just agreed to something that would either make my career or end it.

I sat back down and opened the journal properly, starting from the beginning. The first entry was dated six months ago, and it started with a single line that made my blood run cold:

"They're going to kill me, but first they're going to use me."

Rain continued to hammer against the window. I poured that fourth bourbon after all.

The game was afoot, as they say. And I had a feeling that before this was over, I was going to wish I'd never heard the name Margaret Winters—or The Assembly, or any of the other secrets buried in this city like bodies in unmarked graves.

The cafe seemed to fade away, and Marcus saw it clearly: the mountain rising impossibly high, its summit touching clouds that weren't quite clouds, its slopes hiding secrets that had waited millennia to be discovered.

But that's the thing about being a detective. You can't resist a mystery, even when you know it might kill you.

I raised my glass in a silent toast to the woman in black, wherever she was hiding in this rain-soaked city. Then I started reading, and didn't stop until the sun came up and the rain finally quit, leaving the streets steaming like the city itself was exhaling its sins.

By then, I knew three things for certain:

One, Margaret Winters had been playing a dangerous game with dangerous people.

Two, whoever had walked into my office last night was in deeper trouble than she knew.

And three, that dead man in the hotel room was just the beginning.

The midnight city held its secrets close, but I was James Rook, and prying open secrets was what I did best.

Even if it killed me.
