The neon signs reflected off the rain-slicked streets of New Angeles like electric dreams bleeding into reality. I jacked out of the network at 23:47, my neural implant still buzzing with the afterglow of a successful hack. Three million credits transferred from a corporate account that would never miss it, routed through enough ghost servers that tracing it would take years.

I was Zero, and I was the best.

My apartment was a coffin-sized cube in the Stacks—two hundred stories of humanity crammed into the sky, each pod barely large enough to sleep and jack in. But it was home, and more importantly, it was off the grid. No corporate eyes, no government surveillance. Just me and my rig.

I was peeling off the neural interface when my illegal comms channel pinged. That shouldn't have been possible. I'd coded those channels myself, encrypted them beyond anything the corps could crack. I'd coded those channels myself, encrypted them beyond anything the corps could crack. The only people who knew how to reach me were clients who paid premium for anonymity.

The message was simple: "Zero. I know what you did. I know where you are. And I have a job that will either make you rich beyond your dreams or get you killed. Probably both. Interested? —Cipher"

Cipher. The name every hacker in New Angeles knew and no one had ever met. The ghost in the machine, the legend who supposedly pulled off the Great Blackout of '49, shutting down every network on the West Coast for seventy-two hours. If Cipher was real—and that was a big if—they were my hero.

Or my worst nightmare.

I should have deleted the message. Should have packed up my gear and found a new cube in a different Stack. But curiosity is a hacker's curse, and I'd never been good at walking away from a mystery.

I typed back: "Listening."

The response was instant: "Shibuya Noodle Bar. Sublevel 7. Booth in the back. Come alone. Come now. Burn this message."

Then the message self-destructed, wiping itself from my system with military-grade deletion protocols that I definitely didn't teach it.

I grabbed my jacket—armored synth-leather with built-in scrambler tech—and headed out. Sublevel 7 was deep enough to be dangerous, shallow enough not to be suicide. The kind of place where deals went down and questions didn't get asked.

The Shibuya was exactly what I expected: steam, synthetic meat smells, and the kind of clientele that paid cash and didn't make eye contact. I found the back booth easily enough. It was occupied by someone wearing an optical camouflage hood that blurred their features into static.

"Zero," the voice was processed, unidentifiable. "Sit."

I sat, hand on the EMP device in my pocket. If this went south, I wanted options.

"Relax," Cipher said, and I could hear amusement in the processed voice. "If I wanted you dead, you'd have flatlined in your sleep. I need you alive. More importantly, I need you angry."

"Angry about what?"

Cipher slid a data chip across the table. "Upload this. But not here. Not on any device connected to any network. Air-gapped only."

"What's on it?"

"Proof," Cipher said. "That everything you think you know about memory trading is a lie. That Neuraltech Corporation isn't just selling memories—they're stealing consciousness itself. That everyone who's ever bought a memory implant has a backdoor in their brain, and someone's been using it."

My blood went cold. Neuraltech was the biggest corp in New Angeles. Half the city had their implants. Including—I reached up to touch my own neural interface—me.

"Why tell me?"

"Because three days ago, I tried to expose them. I compiled evidence, prepared to go public, contacted journalists and activists. Then..." Cipher's hand trembled slightly, "then they deleted my entire identity. My face, my fingerprints, my DNA from medical databases. They erased my memories from everyone I ever knew. As far as the world is concerned, I don't exist. I never existed."

"That's impossible."

"Is it? Look at the chip, Zero. Then tell me what's impossible."

I took the chip, feeling its weight like a loaded gun. "What do you want from me?"

"I want you to finish what I started. Find the proof. Expose Neuraltech. Burn the whole corrupt system down. And—" Cipher's voice dropped lower, "I want you to find out who I was. Before they erased me."

"That's a suicide mission."

"Yes. But you'll do it anyway. Because you're like me, Zero. You can't resist a locked door. You have to know what's on the other side, even if it kills you."

Cipher stood to leave, and I grabbed their arm. "How do I contact you? If I find something?"

"You don't. If you succeed, everyone will know. If you fail..." the processed voice carried a hint of sadness, "if you fail, it won't matter. They'll erase you too, and we'll both be ghosts."

Then Cipher was gone, dissolving into the crowd like they'd never been there.

I sat alone in the booth, the data chip burning a hole in my pocket, my mind racing. My implant—the same technology that let me jack into any system, crack any code—might be a weapon pointed at my brain. Everyone I knew, everyone in this city with a Neuraltech implant, might be compromised.

I should have walked away. Thrown the chip in the nearest recycler and forgotten this conversation.

But I was Zero. And Cipher was right—I couldn't resist a mystery.

I left the Shibuya and headed for my backup location: an old server farm on sublevel 15, abandoned and off-network. I had an ancient laptop there, completely air-gapped, perfect for viewing data too dangerous to expose to the network.

The chip loaded slowly, its encryption military-grade. When it finally opened, I started reading, and my world shattered.

Personnel files. Test results. Video footage of people screaming as their memories were stripped away. Financial records showing payments to government officials. And at the center of it all, something called Project Consensus: a plan to link every Neuraltech implant into a single hive mind, with control centralized at corporate headquarters.

They weren't just selling memories. They were building a weapon to control human consciousness itself.

And I had their technology jacked directly into my brain.

I don't know how long I sat there, reading file after file, watching horror after horror unfold in cold corporate documentation. Dawn was breaking over New Angeles when I finally closed the laptop, my decision made.

Cipher was right. This had to be exposed. Even if it killed me.

I had the skills. I had the evidence. What I needed now was a plan, allies, and enough firepower—digital and physical—to take on the most powerful corporation in the city.

I looked out at the neon-soaked sprawl of New Angeles, at the millions of people walking around with Neuraltech implants, unaware they were one command away from becoming puppets.

"Okay, Cipher," I said to the empty server farm. "Let's burn it all down."

My neural implant hummed against my skull, and I wondered if they were already watching, already knew what I'd learned.

But it didn't matter anymore.

I was Zero, the best hacker in New Angeles.

And I had just declared war on the future itself.

The neon dreams were about to become a nightmare, and I was going to make sure everyone woke up.
